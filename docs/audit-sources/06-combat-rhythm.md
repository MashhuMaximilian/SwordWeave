Combat does not place every creature into a rigid initiative queue and force the table to wait while actions resolve one person at a time.

A fight is happening all at once.

Blades are moving, spells are forming, allies are shouting warnings, and enemies are reacting to the same collapsing situation. To represent this, combat is organized through a shared rhythm of **Intent, Complexity, and Reaction**.

Each combat round follows this sequence:

**[1. The Council Phase]**

↓

**[2. Assign Complexity Tracks]**

↓

**[3. Resolve Fast → Measured → Heavy]**

↓

**[4. Trigger Clashes, Contests, and Reactions as Needed]**

### **1. The Council Phase**

At the beginning of every combat round, the table pauses briefly.

During this pause, every player openly declares their character's **Main Intent** in plain, natural language. Players are encouraged to coordinate, warn one another, and construct a shared tactical plan rather than hiding their actions behind individual turns.

At the same time, the GM establishes the visible focus and general intent of the opposing forces.

Players do not need to describe every mechanical component immediately. They only need to establish what their character is attempting to accomplish.

- "I rush the bridge and hold back the armored guard."
- "I create a wall of fire behind him so he cannot retreat."
- "I pull the wounded scout away from the ledge."
- "I begin folding the chamber inward to trap the creature."

The Council Phase is not a lengthy strategy meeting. It is a brief snapshot of everyone's intentions at the opening of the round.

Once every Main Intent is understood, the GM determines when each action enters the fiction.

### **2. Assigning the Complexity Tracks**

The GM evaluates the **Complexity** of each declared intent.

Complexity does more than influence Strain. During combat, it also determines how quickly an action can manifest inside the shared scene.

Actions are placed into one of three chronological tracks:

### **The Fast Track — Complexity 0–1**

Immediate, instinctive, or mechanically simple actions.

Examples include:

- Moving a short distance.
- Drawing or using a simple item.
- Making a direct weapon strike.
- Diving behind nearby cover.
- Shoving an adjacent creature.
- Performing a basic reflexive maneuver.

These actions enter the fiction first.

### **The Measured Track — Complexity 2–3**

Actions requiring deliberate execution, multiple connected movements, or the stable use of a standard capability.

Examples include:

- Activating a prepared capability preset.
- Combining movement with a secondary effect.
- Manipulating part of the environment.
- Creating a standard barrier or area effect.
- Executing a coordinated tactical maneuver.

These actions resolve after the Fast Track.

### **The Heavy Track — Complexity 4+**

Actions that require extended concentration, massive exertion, or extreme pressure against reality.

Examples include:

- Restructuring a large section of the battlefield.
- Channeling a devastating scene-altering capability.
- Summoning a complex entity.
- Collapsing or rewriting spatial boundaries.
- Compressing a ritual-scale effect into a few seconds.

These actions resolve last, provided the character is still capable of completing them.

The tracks do not measure how impressive an action is. They measure how much must happen before the intent can fully enter reality.

A sword thrust may resolve before a dimensional prison—not because the sword is stronger, but because it is already moving while the prison is still being constructed.

### **3. Resolving Actions Within a Track**

Once all actions have been assigned, resolve them in order:

**Fast Track → Measured Track → Heavy Track**

When multiple characters occupy the same track, do not automatically roll for initiative. Use the following three rules.

### **Rule I: Symmetrical Flow**

If only allies are acting within a track, the players choose the order that best supports their coordinated intent.

If only adversaries are acting within a track, the GM resolves them in the order that best reflects the fiction.

No speed roll is required.

Characters on the same side are not racing one another unless the narrative gives them a reason to do so. Their actions simply unfold in the clearest and most dramatic sequence.

### **Rule II: The Reaction Clash**

When an ally's intent and an adversary's intent directly collide within the same track—and the outcome depends on who acts first—a **Reaction Clash** occurs.

Examples include:

- Two combatants lunging for the same weapon.
- An archer firing as a target dives behind cover.
- A duelist striking before an enemy can pull a lever.
- A spellcaster attempting to raise a barrier before an incoming attack lands.

Both sides roll:

**Reaction Clash = 1d20 + Relevant Attribute Modifier + Proficiency Bonus, if trained**

The higher result acts first.

If the rolls are tied, the GM resolves the actions simultaneously or allows the side with the stronger fictional position to act first.

The losing character does not automatically lose their entire action. If their original intent is no longer possible or useful, they may use the **Pivot Point** to immediately adjust it before it resolves.

They may reduce its scale, redirect its target, accept a different cost, or convert it into a defensive response that still makes sense within the fiction.

### **Rule III: The Active Contest**

Not every collision is a race.

When two opposing intents create a direct physical, mental, or magical struggle, resolve the conflict as an **Active Contest** rather than a Reaction Clash.

Examples include:

- Two creatures wrestling over the same object.
- One combatant holding a door while another forces it open.
- Two psychics attempting to dominate the same mind.
- A counterspell pushing directly against an active magical effect.
- Opposing forces attempting to move the same target in different directions.

Each side makes a standard resolution roll using the Attribute, Skill, Capability, or equipment most relevant to their approach.

**Active Contest = 1d20 + Relevant Attribute Modifier + applicable Proficiency and modifiers**

The higher result wins the struggle and dictates the immediate narrative outcome.

A Reaction Clash asks:

**"Who gets there first?"**

An Active Contest asks:

**"Whose intent overpowers the other?"**

### **4. The Independent Reaction Slot**

In addition to their Main Intent, every character possesses exactly **one Reaction Slot per combat round**.

The Reaction Slot resets at the beginning of each Council Phase.

A reaction is not part of your Main Intent and does not depend on whether your Main Intent has already resolved. It is an independent resource used when the fiction suddenly demands an immediate response.

A reaction may be triggered when:

- An enemy attacks a nearby ally.
- A trap suddenly activates.
- A creature attempts to flee past you.
- A companion begins falling.
- A projectile crosses your reach.
- The environment changes without warning.
- A new threat enters the scene.

You cannot spend a reaction merely because you dislike an outcome. A clear narrative catalyst must occur, and your character must be physically, mentally, or magically capable of perceiving and answering it.

### **Reaction Limits**

A reaction represents a fraction of a second. It must therefore remain narrow, immediate, and focused.

By default, a reaction is limited to:

- **Scale:** Self / Touch or Single Target.
- **Complexity:** 0–1 (this could vary depending on the rule of cool and dm's preferences)
- **Timing:** Immediate enough to answer the triggering event.
- **Fiction:** Logically possible from the character's current position and circumstances.

Valid reactions might include:

- Raising a shield.
- Pulling an adjacent ally aside.
- Making a quick retaliatory strike.
- Catching a falling object.
- Creating a brief flash of fire.
- Taking a single step into the path of an attack.
- Disrupting a nearby creature's concentration.

A reaction normally cannot create a large area effect, perform a long sequence of actions, summon a complex entity, or rewrite the battlefield.

However, the **Rule of Cool remains the ultimate law of the table**.

If the fiction strongly supports a more ambitious reaction, the GM may permit it by increasing its Strain, imposing a severe Cost, reducing its effect, or introducing an immediate complication. A reaction may bend its limits, but it never escapes consequence.

### **The Round Ends**

The round ends after all three Complexity Tracks have resolved and all triggered reactions have been completed.

A new Council Phase then begins.

The battlefield is reassessed, Reaction Slots reset, and every participant declares a new Main Intent based on the consequences of the previous round.

Combat therefore remains what the rest of the system promises:

Not a spreadsheet of isolated turns, but a continuous negotiation between intention, pressure, consequence, and the shared fiction of the table.
