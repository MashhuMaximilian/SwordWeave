## Core Principle

Combat does not divide the scene into isolated individual turns.

Instead, every character participates in a shared combat round by declaring one primary intent, moving as part of that intent, and responding to sudden events through one independent reaction.

The Initiative System determines **when** an intent resolves.

The Action Economy determines **how much** a character may attempt during the round.

---

## The Round Economy

During each combat round, every character receives:

- **One Main Intent**
- **One Reaction Slot**
- **Movement as part of their intent**
- Any ongoing effects, upkeep obligations, or passive capabilities currently active

These resources reset at the beginning of the next Council Phase unless a rule states otherwise.

---

## 1. The Main Intent

A character's Main Intent is the primary thing they attempt to accomplish during the round.

It may include:

- A physical attack.
- Activating a capability.
- Manipulating the environment.
- Defending a position.
- Assisting an ally.
- Moving through the battlefield.
- Performing a linked sequence of actions.
- Beginning or completing a complex channelled effect.

The player describes the entire intent in plain, natural language.

They do not divide it into separate categories such as Action, Bonus Action, and Movement.

### Example

Do not say:

"I move thirty feet, use my action to attack, and use a bonus action to disengage."

Say:

"I sprint past the guard, slash at his exposed side, and continue behind the stone pillar before he can surround me."

The GM then evaluates the complete intent using the 3 Dials.

---

## 2. Movement Is Part of Intent

Movement is not a separate action resource.

A character may move whenever movement is a logical part of what they are attempting to accomplish.

- Closing the distance before striking.
  Examples include:
- Retreating behind cover after firing.
- Leaping across a gap.
- Circling an enemy while defending.
- Dragging an injured ally out of danger.
- Climbing to reach a target.
- Repositioning while channeling a capability.

Ordinary movement usually adds little or no Complexity.

Movement may increase Complexity when it introduces a meaningful additional challenge, such as:

- Crossing dangerous terrain.
- Moving through hostile reach.
- Climbing while under attack.
- Performing acrobatics during another action.
- Carrying another creature.
- Travelling an unusually long distance.
- Combining precise movement with several additional effects.

Movement is therefore free to describe, but it is never free from the fiction.

---

## 3. Linked Actions

A Main Intent may contain more than one physical step as long as those steps form one coherent objective.

A character might:

- Run forward and strike.
- Draw a weapon and throw it.
- Leap from a balcony and attack while falling.
- Push an enemy away from an ally and take their place.
- Raise a barrier while retreating.
- Ignite an object and throw it into a group.

The GM does not count verbs.

Instead, the GM asks:

**"Is this one connected intent, or several independent outcomes being compressed together?"**

If every part of the action contributes directly to one objective, it may resolve as a single intent.

If the character is attempting several unrelated outcomes, the GM may:

- Increase Complexity.
- Increase Strain or Cost.
- Reduce the scope of the intent.
- Split the intent into sequential resolutions.
- Ask the player to choose which result matters most.

### Example: Connected Intent

"I charge the guard and drive him away from the doorway."

The movement and attack support one outcome: removing the guard from the entrance.

### Example: Multiple Independent Outcomes

"I attack the guard, unlock the prison door, grab the prisoner, and throw a smoke bomb at the archers."

These are several separate objectives. The GM should increase the stakes, split the resolution, or require the player to narrow the intent.

---

## 4. Complexity and Action Scope

The system does not balance combat through rigid action categories.

The scope of a Main Intent is regulated through the Complexity Dial.

### Complexity 0–1: Simple Intent

One direct effect or a brief sequence of naturally connected movements.

Examples:

- Strike a nearby enemy.
- Move behind cover.
- Draw and throw a dagger.
- Pull an ally out of immediate danger.
- Raise a shield against an obvious threat.

These intents normally enter the Fast Track.

### Complexity 2–3: Layered Intent

Two or more linked elements, a standard capability assembly, or meaningful interaction with the environment.

Examples:

- Attack while repositioning through hostile space.
- Create a barrier and use it to divide two enemies.
- Freeze the floor beneath a charging creature.
- Teleport to an ally and carry them to safety.

These intents normally enter the Measured Track.

### Complexity 4+: Heavy Intent

Several interacting effects, extreme compression of time, or major pressure against physical or magical reality.

Examples:

- Collapse a tower while shielding nearby allies.
- Freeze a river and reshape it into a bridge.
- Teleport several creatures through different locations.
- Create a scene-wide storm while maintaining another effect.

These intents normally enter the Heavy Track.

Complexity determines both the pressure of the action and its place within the Combat Rhythm.

There is no separate action-speed tag.

## 5. The Reaction Slot

Every character has one Reaction Slot per round.

The Reaction Slot is independent of the character's Main Intent. It may be used before or after the Main Intent resolves.

A reaction must answer a clear event occurring within the fiction.

Possible triggers include:

- An enemy attacks an ally.
- A creature moves past the character.
- A trap activates.
- A projectile enters reach.
- An ally falls.
- A spell begins forming.
- A nearby structure collapses.
- A creature attempts to escape.

Possible reactions include:

- Dodge.
- Block.
- Counter.
- Interrupt.
- Catch.
- Pull.
- Make a brief retaliatory strike.
- Activate a defensive capability.
- Move a very short distance in direct response to danger.

A character cannot use a reaction merely to perform a second planned Main Intent.

Reactions are triggered responses, not additional turns.

---

## 6. Reaction Scope

By default, a reaction is limited to:

- **Scale:** Self / Touch or Single Target.
- **Complexity:** 0–1.
- **Duration:** Immediate.
- **Purpose:** Directly responding to the triggering event.

A reaction may include a small amount of reflexive movement when necessary, such as stepping into the path of an attack or pulling an adjacent ally aside.

A reaction should not normally:

- Affect a large area.
- Create several independent effects.
- Require long concentration.
- Perform a full tactical sequence.
- Duplicate the scope of a Main Intent.
- Resolve an action that was clearly planned during the Council Phase.

The GM may permit an exceptional reaction when strongly supported by the fiction, but should impose additional Strain, Cost, reduced effect, or a dangerous complication.

---

## 7. Reaction Timing

A reaction interrupts the current narrative moment.

When a reaction is declared:

1. Pause the triggering action.
1. Confirm that the reaction is possible.
1. Evaluate its Complexity, Strain, and Cost.
1. Resolve any required Clash, Contest, or standard roll.
1. Continue resolving the triggering action according to the new circumstances.

A reaction does not automatically cancel its trigger.

It changes the situation, and the fiction determines what remains possible.

### Example

An enemy fires an arrow at an ally.

A character uses their Reaction Slot to raise a kinetic barrier.

If the barrier succeeds, the arrow may be stopped or deflected.

If the barrier fails, the attack continues normally.

The reaction was still spent.

---

## 8. Maintained and Channelled Actions

Some intents cannot fully resolve within a single moment.

A character may begin a channelled action as their Main Intent. Until it resolves, they may need to:

- Maintain concentration.
- Pay an upkeep Cost.
- Remain in position.
- Protect a focus or component.
- Avoid interruption.

A channelled action remains part of the character's Main Intent for that round.

It does not grant additional actions simply because its final effect has not yet manifested.

If the character is disrupted before completion, the GM determines whether the intent:

- Fails.
- Resolves at reduced strength.
- Requires an additional Cost.
- Continues into the next round.
- Can be redirected using the Pivot Point.

---

## 9. No Hidden Action Categories

The system does not use:

- Standard Actions.
- Bonus Actions.
- Move Actions.
- Free Object Interactions.
- Full-Round Actions.
- Minor Actions.

These categories are replaced by one question:

**"What is your character trying to accomplish during this round?"**

The GM evaluates the answer through Scale, Impact, Complexity, Strain, Cost, and the established fiction.

Simple actions remain fast and inexpensive.

Layered actions become slower or more demanding.

Extreme actions remain possible, but reality demands an appropriate price.

---

## 10. Combat Flow Integration

The complete combat sequence is:

### The Council Phase

- Reaction Slots reset.
- Players declare their Main Intents.
- The GM establishes adversary intents.
- The table clarifies positioning and coordination.

### Evaluation

- The GM reads the 3 Dials.
- Strain and Cost are established.
- Each Main Intent is placed into a Complexity Track.

### Resolution

- Fast Track resolves.
- Measured Track resolves.
- Heavy Track resolves.
- Clashes and Active Contests resolve direct conflicts.
- Reactions interrupt whenever their triggers occur.

### End of Round

- Resolve end-of-round effects.
- Apply ongoing consequences.
- Pay or note required upkeep.
- Begin the next Council Phase.

## Core Balance Principle

The action economy is not balanced by restricting characters to artificial mechanical slots.

It is balanced through:

- Complexity.
- Strain.
- Vitality Cost.
- Capability ownership.
- Positioning.
- Narrative consequence.
- Reaction limits.
- The time required for an intent to manifest.

Capabilities define what a character has mastered.

The Action Economy defines how those capabilities enter the shared scene.

The Initiative System determines when they arrive.

Together, these systems preserve cinematic freedom without allowing every intent to become an unlimited chain of unrelated effects.
