# 🧠 PRACTICE SYSTEM

## Player-Facing Interaction Layer

---

## Core Identity

Practices are the **player-facing language layer** used for ordinary interaction with the world.

They describe the broad areas in which a character has training, experience, or refined competence.

Practices act as the interface between:

- Attributes: Physical, Mental, and Magical/Abstract
- Proficiency Bonus
- Build Unit investments
- Capabilities
- The fictional situation

Practices do not define everything a character is allowed to attempt.

> Practices describe what a character performs reliably, not what they are permitted to try.

Any character may attempt to climb, persuade, investigate, track, interpret magic, or understand another creature.

Training determines consistency and expertise.

---

# Design Principle

Practices are not a separate action system.

They are:

> Broad interpretations of ordinary checks.

Players still begin by declaring intent in natural language.

The GM then determines:

1. Which Attribute governs the attempt.
1. Whether one of the character's Practices applies.
1. Whether a Capability or situational modifier changes the attempt.
1. Whether a roll is necessary.

Practices are not buttons that players activate.

They are broad competency categories used to interpret the declared action.

---

# The Ten Core Practices

Practices are permanently associated with one of the three Core Attributes.

The distribution is intentionally asymmetrical.

- Physical characters receive much of their identity from combat, movement, and physical Capabilities.
- Mental characters rely more heavily on learned competence, social interaction, and ordinary problem-solving.
- Magical/Abstract characters receive much of their identity from Domains and supernatural Capabilities.

---

# 🔴 Physical Practices

Physical Practices govern direct bodily execution and practical interaction with the material environment.

## Prowess

Prowess represents strength, athletic performance, endurance, bodily force, and physical resistance.

Use Prowess when the central question is:

> Can the character generate or withstand enough physical force?

Prowess may include:

- Lifting, pushing, pulling, or breaking
- Climbing through strength or endurance
- Swimming against dangerous currents
- Running and jumping
- Grappling or restraining
- Resisting forced movement
- Enduring pain, exhaustion, deprivation, or severe weather
- Sustaining prolonged physical exertion

Examples:

- Holding a collapsing gate open
- Wrestling an enemy away from a lever
- Swimming through a flooded tunnel
- Breaking down a reinforced door
- Continuing to march after extreme exhaustion

Prowess does not automatically replace combat Capabilities or attack rolls.

It governs physical actions surrounding combat and exploration when no constructed Capability is resolving the action directly.

---

## Finesse

Finesse represents precision, coordination, balance, controlled movement, subtlety, and delicate physical execution.

Use Finesse when the central question is:

> Can the character control their body precisely enough?

Finesse may include:

- Acrobatics and balance
- Stealth and silent movement
- Sleight of hand
- Lock manipulation
- Escaping restraints
- Delicate manual work
- Navigating unstable or narrow surfaces
- Precise bodily timing
- Concealing a physical action

Examples:

- Crossing a narrow beam
- Taking a key without being noticed
- Moving silently across broken glass
- Picking a mechanical lock
- Catching a fragile object before it falls
- Slipping free from tightly bound rope

Finesse describes controlled physical execution. It does not automatically provide knowledge of the environment or explain how a mechanism works.

---

## Fieldcraft

Fieldcraft represents practical experience with terrain, wilderness, travel, tracking, weather, and survival.

Use Fieldcraft when the central question is:

> Does the character know how to operate effectively in this environment?

Fieldcraft may include:

- Tracking creatures or people
- Concealing or identifying trails
- Navigation
- Hunting and foraging
- Reading weather and terrain
- Selecting safe routes
- Establishing camps and shelters
- Preparing snares or practical wilderness tools
- Identifying environmental hazards through experience
- Managing prolonged journeys

Examples:

- Following tracks through a forest
- Finding a safe path across unstable ground
- Predicting an approaching storm
- Concealing the party's campsite
- Determining where an animal is likely to find water
- Establishing shelter before nightfall

Fieldcraft is practical and experiential.

It does not cover academic ecological knowledge, supernatural communication with nature, or magical manipulation of terrain.

---

# 🔵 Mental Practices

Mental Practices govern observation, reasoning, stored knowledge, communication, and deliberate social interaction.

The four Mental Practices form an information cycle:

> Awareness notices.

---

## Awareness

Awareness represents attention to immediate, observable reality.

Use Awareness when the central question is:

> What does the character notice?

Awareness may include:

- Seeing, hearing, or smelling subtle details
- Detecting hidden creatures or objects
- Searching an area
- Recognizing immediate danger
- Maintaining vigilance
- Noticing physical inconsistencies
- Detecting changes in the environment
- Observing visible behavior

Examples:

- Hearing movement behind a door
- Spotting disturbed dust near a floor tile
- Noticing someone reaching for a concealed weapon
- Finding a hidden compartment
- Detecting an approaching ambush
- Smelling smoke before seeing the fire

Awareness discovers information.

It does not automatically explain what the information means.

---

## Reason

Reason represents deduction, interpretation of evidence, technical analysis, diagnosis, and intellectual problem-solving.

Use Reason when the central question is:

> What conclusion can the character derive from the available information?

Reason may include:

- Investigation and deduction
- Deciphering codes
- Reconstructing past events
- Diagnosing injuries or unfamiliar illnesses
- Understanding mechanisms
- Comparing contradictory accounts
- Identifying structural weaknesses
- Interpreting complex written arguments
- Solving character-facing intellectual problems

Examples:

- Reconstructing how a murder occurred
- Determining how a machine operates
- Breaking a written cipher
- Diagnosing an unfamiliar poison
- Finding the contradiction between two testimonies
- Determining why a damaged bridge is unstable

### The Planning Rule

Reason does not create plans for the player.

Players decide what their characters attempt.

A Reason check may provide relevant information, identify constraints, or reveal likely consequences, but it does not tell the player the optimal action.

Do not roll Reason to answer:

> What should we do?

Roll Reason to answer:

> What can my character determine before we decide what to do?

---

## Knowledge

Knowledge represents education, memory, study, research, and accumulated factual familiarity.

Use Knowledge when the central question is:

> Does the character already know something relevant?

Knowledge may include:

- History
- Geography
- Cultures and institutions
- Laws and customs
- Languages
- Anatomy and medical theory
- Academic subjects
- Engineering principles
- Recognizing symbols, people, locations, or historical events
- Familiarity with mundane professions and technologies

Examples:

- Recalling which dynasty constructed a fortress
- Recognizing the symptoms of a known disease
- Remembering a kingdom's burial customs
- Identifying the language used in a manuscript
- Knowing the legal authority of a noble title
- Recognizing the uniform of a historical military order

### Knowledge versus Reason

Knowledge retrieves existing information.

Reason produces a conclusion from available information.

- Knowledge recognizes a known poison.
- Reason diagnoses an unfamiliar poison from its effects.
- Knowledge identifies the language in a book.
- Reason deciphers the code hidden inside the text.
- Knowledge recalls who built the fortress.
- Reason determines why its wall is collapsing.

They may interact, but they answer different questions.

---

## Influence

Influence represents deliberate control over communication, presentation, attention, trust, emotion, fear, and social response.

Use Influence when the central question is:

> Can the character cause another person or audience to respond as intended?

Influence may include:

- Persuasion
- Deception
- Intimidation
- Performance
- Negotiation
- Leadership
- Reassurance
- Social misdirection
- Commanding attention
- Concealing emotional intent

Examples:

- Convincing a guard to allow passage
- Maintaining a false identity
- Frightening an enemy into surrender
- Inspiring frightened civilians
- Negotiating a trade agreement
- Distracting a crowd through performance
- Calming an angry official

Influence does not make every social method identical.

The declared intent determines the fictional method and consequences.

- Persuasion may create genuine agreement.
- Deception creates a false belief and risks discovery.
- Intimidation creates compliance but may cause fear or resentment.
- Performance shapes attention or emotion.

The Practice is shared. The narrative result is not.

---

# 🟣 Magical / Abstract Practices

Magical/Abstract Practices govern supernatural understanding, spiritual or ecological connection, and sensitivity to hidden meaning.

These Practices do not grant magical effects.

Actual supernatural actions are constructed through Domains, Verbs, Capabilities, items, or other sources of narrative permission.

---

## Mysticism

Mysticism represents understanding of magical systems, supernatural forces, metaphysical structures, and reality-altering phenomena.

Use Mysticism when the central question is:

> What supernatural structure or force is operating here?

Mysticism may include:

- Magical theory
- Arcane symbols
- Enchantments and curses
- Supernatural anomalies
- Ritual structures
- Magical items
- Domains and metaphysical forces
- Wards and portals
- Dimensional phenomena
- Reality distortions

Examples:

- Identifying the function of a magical ward
- Determining which Domain shaped an effect
- Understanding the structure of a ritual
- Recognizing that an item is cursed
- Investigating a spatial anomaly
- Interpreting an arcane diagram

Mysticism allows a character to recognize or understand magic.

It does not automatically allow the character to cast, dispel, or reproduce the effect.

---

## Communion

Communion represents relationship, attunement, and understanding involving living, ecological, spiritual, or sacred systems.

Use Communion when the central question is:

> How does the character understand or relate to this being, presence, tradition, or living system?

Communion may include:

- Animal behavior
- Plants and ecosystems
- Spirits and ancestors
- Religious practice
- Sacred traditions
- Divine presences
- Rituals as lived spiritual acts
- Calming or understanding nonhuman creatures
- Recognizing ecological or spiritual imbalance
- Interpreting sacred customs

Examples:

- Calming a frightened animal
- Understanding the behavior of a supernatural beast
- Recognizing that a forest has become spiritually disturbed
- Interpreting the meaning of a funeral rite
- Identifying whether a shrine is still spiritually active
- Recognizing signs associated with a local spirit

### Communion versus Knowledge

Knowledge recalls factual information about a being, religion, or ecosystem.

Communion understands its relationships, behavior, sacred meaning, or living context.

- Knowledge recalls when a temple was constructed.
- Communion understands what its ritual means to worshippers.
- Knowledge identifies an animal species.
- Communion understands why the animal is distressed.
- Knowledge knows the recorded doctrine of a faith.
- Communion recognizes whether a sacred presence is responding.

---

## Intuition

Intuition represents sensitivity to emotion, motive, hidden meaning, resonance, omens, and patterns that are not fully visible or logically established.

Use Intuition when the central question is:

> What lies beneath what is being shown?

Intuition may include:

- Reading emotional states
- Sensing concealed motives
- Recognizing dishonesty or unease
- Interpreting dreams and omens
- Feeling supernatural resonance
- Recognizing meaningful patterns
- Sensing that something is fundamentally wrong
- Empathic understanding

Examples:

- Sensing that someone's anger is performed
- Recognizing that a character is afraid for someone else
- Feeling that an apparently ordinary room is spiritually wrong
- Interpreting the emotional meaning of a dream
- Recognizing tension between two people
- Sensing that an offer carries hidden desperation

Intuition reveals impressions, emotional truths, or hidden pressures.

It does not provide perfect factual certainty or automatic lie detection.

A successful Intuition check may reveal:

> His confidence feels forced. He is concealing fear.

It should not automatically reveal:

> He murdered the duke at midnight using a poisoned knife.

# Practice Boundary Summary

Use these questions when several Practices appear relevant.

| Practice | Core Question |
| --- | --- |
| Prowess (P) | Can I generate or withstand enough physical force? |
| Finesse (P) | Can I execute this precisely or discreetly? |
| Fieldcraft (P) | Can I operate effectively in this environment? |
| Awareness (M) | What do I notice? |
| Reason (M) | What can I determine from the available information? |
| Knowledge (M) | What does my character already know? |
| Influence (M) | Can I shape another person's response? |
| Mysticism (A) | What supernatural structure is operating here? |
| Communion (A) | How do I relate to this living, sacred, or spiritual presence? |
| Intuition (A) | What hidden meaning or emotion lies beneath the surface? |

# Mechanical Role

Practices are used when an action contains meaningful uncertainty and a Practice is relevant to the declared approach.

Examples:

- "I force the door open." → Prowess
- "I cross the beam without falling." → Finesse
- "I follow the tracks into the hills." → Fieldcraft
- "I search the desk for a hidden mechanism." → Awareness
- "I determine how the mechanism works." → Reason
- "I recall who designed mechanisms like this." → Knowledge
- "I convince the owner to explain it." → Influence
- "I identify the magic affecting it." → Mysticism
- "I ask the spirit bound to it what happened." → Communion or a Capability
- "I sense what emotion was impressed into it." → Intuition

Not every action requires a roll.

Do not roll when:

- The outcome is already certain.
- Failure would not create a meaningful consequence.
- The character has sufficient time and ability to succeed automatically.
- A Capability directly resolves the obstacle without meaningful resistance.

# Resolution Stack

When a Practice applies, use the existing unified resolution system:

```
Result =
d20
+ Relevant Attribute Modifier
+ Attribute Proficiency Bonus, if applicable
+ Practice Proficiency Bonus, if trained
+ BU-purchased passive modifiers, if applicable
+ Capability or item modifiers, if relevant
+ Situational modifiers
```

Practice Expertise replaces the normal Practice Proficiency contribution with double Proficiency Bonus.

```
Practice Proficiency = +PB

Practice Expertise = +2 × PB
```

Attribute Proficiency and Practice Proficiency are separate sources under the current system and may both contribute when applicable.

---

# Practice and DC Separation

Practices normally modify active checks.

They do not automatically increase:

- Physical DC
- Mental DC
- Magical DC
- Passive defenses
- Capability resistance
- Damage
- Vitality

Defensive DC remains:

```
Defensive DC =
5
+ Relevant Attribute Modifier
+ Attribute Proficiency Bonus, if trained
+ Explicit defensive modifiers
```

A Practice only modifies a Defensive DC when a specific component explicitly says that it does.

---

# Capability Interaction

Practices govern ordinary competency.

Capabilities govern constructed effects and extraordinary action.

A Capability may interact with a Practice in three ways.

## 1. Direct Resolution

The Capability directly solves the obstacle.

Example:

A character teleports to the top of a wall.

No Prowess or Finesse check is needed unless the teleport itself is contested or unstable.

## 2. Augmented Attempt

The Capability improves an ordinary action.

Example:

A character uses controlled wind to support themselves while climbing.

The action may still use Prowess or Finesse, with an appropriate Capability modifier or reduced Strain.

## 3. New Permission

The Capability allows an action that would otherwise be impossible.

Example:

A character uses Communion and a Life Capability to question the memories held by ancient roots.

The Capability creates the supernatural permission. Communion may interpret or stabilize the interaction.

Capabilities do not automatically replace Practices unless their effect directly resolves the obstacle.

# ⚙️ BU Integration

## Canonical Clarification

BU is a build-time currency.

BU does not enter a roll as an unstructured resource during play.

However, BU may purchase permanent components that affect checks.

Therefore:

> BU is not itself a runtime modifier.

This preserves the separation between the progression economy and action resolution without preventing BU from purchasing Practice training.

---

# BU Practice Component Market

The following prices are provisional playtest values.

## Focus Training

### 2 BU — Passive

Choose one narrow, clearly defined application of a Practice.

When that exact Focus applies, treat the character as proficient for that check.

Focus Training should be /Skillnarrower than an entire former skill.

Valid examples:

- Awareness through smell
- Reason when deciphering ciphers
- Knowledge concerning a specific kingdom
- Influence when negotiating prices
- Fieldcraft when tracking humanoids
- Communion involving domesticated animals
- Mysticism involving magical wards
- Finesse when picking mechanical locks

Invalid examples:

- All Awareness checks
- All social interactions
- All wilderness actions
- All magic
- All investigations

A Focus may later be upgraded into full Practice Proficiency by paying the BU difference.

### Example

**Keen Nose — 2 BU**

The character is treated as proficient in Awareness checks where smell is the primary source of information.

---

## Practice Proficiency

### 4 BU — Passive

Gain proficiency in one complete Practice.

When the Practice applies, add the character's Proficiency Bonus to the check.

Examples:

- Prowess Proficiency
- Influence Proficiency
- Mysticism Proficiency
- Reason Proficiency

Practice Proficiency represents broad, reliable training.

---

## Practice Expertise

### 8 BU total — Passive Upgrade

Requires Practice Proficiency.

The character adds double their Proficiency Bonus when the chosen Practice applies.

If the character already purchased Practice Proficiency for 4 BU, upgrading to Expertise costs an additional 4 BU.

```
Practice Expertise Bonus = 2 × PB
```

Expertise applies to one complete Practice.

---

## Focused Edge

### 3 BU — Augment

Requires proficiency in the relevant Practice.

Choose one narrow and clearly defined Focus.

The character gains Advantage when that Focus directly applies.

Examples:

- Advantage on Awareness checks based primarily on smell
- Advantage on Influence checks when performing for a crowd
- Advantage on Reason checks involving mechanical devices
- Advantage on Fieldcraft checks when navigating mountains
- Advantage on Communion checks involving horses
- Advantage on Mysticism checks involving curses

Focused Edge should describe a recognizable specialization, not a universal condition.

---

## Broad Familiarity

### 8 BU — Passive

The character adds half their Proficiency Bonus, rounded down, to Practice checks in which they are not already proficient.

```
Broad Familiarity Bonus = floor(PB / 2)
```

Broad Familiarity does not stack with Practice Proficiency or Expertise.

Use the highest applicable Practice training bonus.

This represents wide but shallow experience across many areas.

---

## Reliable Practice

### 10 BU — Mastery Upgrade

Requires Expertise in one Practice.

When making a check using the chosen Practice, treat a natural d20 result of 9 or lower as a 10.

Reliable Practice applies only to the selected Practice.

It does not apply to every proficient check the character makes.

Examples:

- Reliable Reason
- Reliable Fieldcraft
- Reliable Influence

This is an advanced mastery component and should be monitored closely during playtesting.

---

## Attribute → Practice Distribution

Each of the three Core Attributes (Physical, Mental, Magical/Abstract) has a numerical modifier ranging from −1 to +5. This modifier is **distributed** to its sub-Practices at character creation, not applied wholesale.

**Example:**

- Physical +5 → distributed as +2 Prowess, +3 Finesse, +0 Fieldcraft
- Mental +5 → distributed as +1 Awareness, +3 Reason, +1 Influence
- Magical -1 → all goes to one Practice (e.g., -1 Mysticism)

**Respec Rule:** Players may fully redistribute their attribute slices at levels **4, 8, 12, 16, and 20** with DM approval, if it makes narrative sense. Outside these milestones, the distribution is locked.

**Implementation:** When making a Practice check, use the sub-attribute modifier assigned to that Practice at character creation (or as last redistributed). Add Proficiency Bonus only when the relevant Attribute is proficient OR when the Practice is specifically marked as proficient.
